evann 04-Dec-21 09:54 PM
It's also worth noting that it's wildly easier for a DAO to buy from another DAO. Because a DAO can swap tokens, and then you can create abstractions upon the DAO that let one governance token control another token. For example, if A owns B, then you can make A-token vote on proposals by B-token, proportional to the ownership. That let's you vote with an entire portfolio of governance tokens, which lets you pool your tokens into single delegate programs that apply the same rules to several different DAOs. For example, in Strangemood (our Steam as a DAO), if we want every sub-DAO to collectively fund a conference, we can abstract away the need to vote for each DAO to fund their portion. That lets DAOs "merge together" with their customers and suppliers without making things harder to coordinate. That desire is quite strong, and a traditional C-corp can realistically just say "lol, here's my whole company, go nuts", it means the DAO is incentivized to buy from the DAO competitor. And since the DAO borg-machine takes no value capture, it's (theoretically) more efficient and cheaper; one big new economy with a network effect

Investors put up collateral to borrow against. When the market moves against their position they're liquidated.
An investor puts up 100 USD to open a 200 USD position. When this position drops 50% they're liquidated.
Investors with a strong track record could trade with more leverage. Skilled investors could also use this system to rapidly build up their track record. cc: @Qworg

Because it is too labor intensive and subjective to come up with a valuation of an early-stage company, one lacking any historical financial data that would be accepted by everyone. This is compounded by the fact that investors would have to do this for every company they entered into due diligence with. Early-stage investors often are individual angels who don't have the skills to value a company, even if they had historical financials. this is why individual valuations usually start at the seed stage and are done by a lead investor who is selected at least in part because they can do valuations. The solution for this is a standardized deal and terms found in a SAFE or in a priced round. Most often done at the pre-seed stage. Yes, there are a range of amounts invested and what percentage of the company given up depending on the investors involved, e.g., YC commands a better deal because of who they are, and the team/traction of the startup. What this does is to create a band that everyone can use to see if their deal falls within "market" or not.
